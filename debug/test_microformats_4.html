FEATURING:  

Ballet San Jose

Hailed by The San Francisco Chronicle as "one of the most entertaining versions of Nutcracker anywhere in the country," and "A masterful approach to storytelling. Bubbles over with with and charm" by the San Jose Mercury News, Dennis Nahat's treasured holiday classic returns from an extended run December 11-26 at San Jose Center for the Performing Arts. This triumphant tale of Maria and her beloved nutcracker will delight audiences, young and old alike: and downtown San Jose's holiday decorations, Outdoor Ice Rink and 'Christmas In The Park' display provide the perfect backdrop for a fun-filled family outing. The theatre itself is a wonderful of surprises, replete with lavish lobby decorations, a boutique of ballet and holiday treasures, choirs singing carols, the 'Pocket Lady' passing out free toys, and characters from the show posing for photos with patrons. A Not-To-Be-Missed Treasure of the Season, and a Gift for the Holidays.

Choreography: Dennis Nahat

Music: Peter Ilyich Tchaikovsky

Ballet San Jose presents three Family Matinee performances of THE NUTCRACER for young audiences (ages 3 and up) and their families at reduced rates of $16-$60, on Saturday, December 11 at 1:30pm, Tuesday, December 21st at 1:30pm and Sunday, December 26th at 1:30pm. These are full-length productions with full sets and costumes. (Performed to a recorded score. All other performances are danced to live orchestra.)

Some Program Notes About The Historical Background On The Original Scenario:

We thought you might be interested to know a few things that make our version of The Nutcracker a bit different from some other productions you may have seen. Many people are familiar with the leading young heroine’s name being “Clara,” while in our production we call her “Maria.” “Maria” is the name given to the character by E.T.A. Hoffmann, author of the original story on which the scenario for The Nutcracker ballet is based. While George Balanchine, the first contemporary choreographer of The Nutcracker, preferred to call his lead character “Clara” and spawned a legion of followers, for our production we prefer to return to the original name for the character.

The story of The Nutcracker that includes the Sugar Plum Fairy and the dances to the theme of candies from different countries is also an adaptation of the original story written by Hoffmann. Hoffmann’s story was an allegorical statement against the government, and in order to get the story out to the public and past the government censors, he had to alter it by changing countries into something less political, such as chocolate and sugar plum fairies. Even Peter Ilyich Tchaikovsky, the composer of the music for The Nutcracker, strenuously objected to the change of scenario and the idea of having a Sugar Plum Fairy, but he submitted to Hoffmann’s wish to remain apolitical. Hence the character of the Tsarina was changed to the Sugar Plum Fairy and the countries to candy. If you are familiar with the music for The Nutcracker, you will notice that the beautiful solo dance that the Tsarina performs in our version is set to the music called “Dance of the Sugar Plum Fairy.” Because Tchaikovsky’s music (although written for Hoffmann’s original story) was immediately adapted to an altered version of the tale, choreographers have felt free to continue to make alterations. To this day there is no definitive scenario.

More...

The Nutcracker, first performed in Russia at the Maryinsky Theater in St. Petersburg on December 18, 1892, is the last of Tchaikovsky’s trio of evening-length ballets. Based on “The Nutcracker and The Mouse King,” a fairytale by the German author E.T.A. Hoffmann, the ballet had a scenario by Marius Petipa, a giant figure among 19th-century choreographers, who was expected to create the choreography as well. Due to illness, he was unable to choreograph the ballet, and the work was assigned to his associate, Lev Ivanov. Substantial portions survive today of Petipa’s original choreography for The Sleeping Beauty (1890) and the choreography he made in collaboration with Ivanov for the highly successful version of Swan Lake (1895). In fact, almost all contemporary settings of these ballets are based in greater or lesser degree upon the original choreography — usually with changes made to accommodate the needs and ideas of individual companies and choreographers. Of Ivanov’s choreography for The Nutcracker, only a little has survived, and the numerous contemporary versions of the work tend to be quite new conceptions.

Ballet San Jose’s Nutcracker has a scenario by Ian Horvath and Dennis Nahat, rather loosely based upon E.T.A. Hoffmann’s story, but with totally new choreography by Nahat. Although the original Nutcracker was first performed during the Christmas season, the ballet is considered primarily a holiday entertainment only in America. Its innumerable productions here attest to the fact that it is uniquely suited to our holiday spirit. The Russian classic has become the perennially popular American holiday ballet.
